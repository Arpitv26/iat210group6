Inspiration Paragraph

The game is loosely inspired by the expedition structure in the 2025 video game Expedition 33, in which a torn city repeatedly sends groups into the world to accomplish a larger survival objective. In our design, each kingdom similarly deploys units to gather scarce resources and secure long-term dominance. However, our implementation shifts this idea into a competitive board-game format centred on spatial control, risk management, and PVP interaction.

We also drew inspiration from Monopoly by including action and reaction cards that introduce randomness and situational shifts, increasing replayability and dynamic decision-making. The game’s unit diversity takes inspiration from the various classes players can choose from in JRPGs. Some games we took inspiration from were the Dragon Quest series and Metaphor ReFantazio. Other aspects of the game, like the tunnel system and the resource-return mechanic, came naturally as we tried to build around factional tension and a cheese-based resource-scarcity type of gameplay.

Three Pillars (What / Why / How)

What?

Compete to secure scarce cheese resources across a shared board

Why?

To eliminate neighbouring Kingdoms to become the Cheese Bearer

How?

Deploy units to scavenge, steal, defend, and fight using cards, dice, and positioning

Thematic Backstory

The Great Fromage Gommage

Long ago, the continent of Pancheesa was a barren wasteland until Mouses descended from the sky, echoing blessings from the Mouse God of Cheese. Under his wisdom, Pancheesa became a land of abundance. Rivers flowed with whey, hills ripened with golden cheddar, and many kingdoms thrived in harmony under the sacred Cheese Treaty that promised every mouse access to cheese. For 100 years, peace reigned.

But peace can only last so long…

From the caves in the icy regions of Pancheesa, the shadow of the Ferious Feline Felix fell across the land. He spouted madness about overconsumption of cheese, and distrust began to spread. Felix ignited fear, paranoia and betrayal for all the kingdoms. The Treaty was shattered. Thus began the Great Fromage Gommage, a devastating war that drained Pancheesa of its once-infinite supply of cheese.

Now, only four kingdoms remain…and cheese is scarce….

To survive, each kingdom must

  • Send out units to gather the remaining cheese
  • Protect their borders from rival kingdoms
  • Strategically eliminate competitors
  • Secure dominance over Pancheesa

Only then, one ruler will claim the title of The Cheese Bearer!

The Four Remaining Kingdoms

The Kingdom by the Sea

Ruled by Socrattus, a philosopher-king who believes strategy is stronger than strength.

The mind games they play on you not only confuse you, but them, too

The Kingdom Beneath the Sands

Ruled by Julius Cheeser, a bold and ambitious tactician

Their forces strike fast and retreat even faster

The Kingdom protected by the Mountains

Ruled by Mouser Teresa, a steadfast defender of tradition

Their strongholds are nearly impossible to conquer, that some have a hard time even leaving.

The Kingdom hidden in the Forest

Ruled by Squeakspeare, a cunning ruler of shadow and whispers.

Master of ambush, deception, and stageplays

The World of Pancheesa

The Undercheese Passageways

The Cheese Tunnels were originally used for trading, diplomacy, and avoiding awkward social interactions…

Ever since the Great Fromage Gommage, the tunnels have been repurposed for surprise attacks, strategic escapes, and cowering away from Felix.

No one remembers who built these tunnel systems. The Guild of Thieves claims credit, but nobody really trusts them.

Hazards of the Land

The current state of Pancheesa is dangerous! The Ferious Feline Felix roams the land, judging everyone silently. The environment gets hit with frequent sandstorms (mostly because Julius Cheeser keeps challenging the wind). Mouse Pirates terrorize the roads with their mateys, waiting to ambush any kingdom unit.

Action and Reaction!

War requires innovation. Luckily, each Kingdom is ready to make use of whatever it can to succeed. Some tactics include:

  • Mouse wheels- Strap yourself in and roll dramatically across the board
  • Carrier Pigeons- Sometimes walking is too much, fly back to your kingdom
  • Mousetraps- Ironically effective when fighting other mice
  • Poison Arrows- Slow enemies and ruin friendships

The Units of Pancheesa

  • Hero Mice: Your standard-issue adventurer. Motivated by glory, destiny, and mild delusion.
  • Backpack Mice: Can carry extra cheese with their XL backpack. Maybe not strong enough to carry the extra piece of cheese, but its kinda too late to complain
  • Thief Mice: These ninja-like mice can avoid traps. They dress like ninjas but live a pretty fashionable lifestyle when not on the clock
  • Warrior Mice: They train daily with their broadsword, so you do not want to be on the other side of their blade. You can also find them working part-time as a barista since the economy is tough.
  • Wizard Mice: Ancient, wise, and slightly dramatic. They have the advantage when being attacked by enemies. They have ties to the creation of the Cheese Treaty, but it's rare to see them nowadays
  • Archer Mice: Deadly from a distance. A bit nervous when up close. Their long-range attacks strike fear into opposing units
  • Knight Mice: Also known as the Three Mouseketeers, they share a strong bond with each other. Difficult to steal from due to their flawless teamwork
  • The Necromicer: A user of forbidden magic. They can revive fallen units and probably have your name written down somewhere…

Dynamics / Trade-Offs / Dilemmas / Typical Playtime

Dynamics

A successful strategy we found was to immediately try and invade somebody else's castle during the first stages of the game. Most players were too preoccupied with racing toward the cheese closest to them, so if one player immediately went for somebody else's castle, there was usually nobody nearby to intercept them. This would knock down the opponent's pre-existing cheese quantity while increasing your own. The Carrier Pigeon and Necromicer cards also became a common strategy because of how effective they were during playtests.

Trade-Offs And Dilemmas

Players have to choose whether they want to risk going over a hazard tile, since there is always a chance they will have to draw from the hazard deck. Tunnels also serve as a strong strategic way to move around the board, but the possibility of triggering a hazard created a recurring dilemma over whether the shortcut was worth the risk.

Typical Playtime

Play sessions usually lasted around 1 hour for players with prior experience. New players took a bit longer because they first had to understand each unit's ability, how combat worked, how hazard and tunnel tiles were used, and the overall board flow.
